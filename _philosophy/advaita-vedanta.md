Advaita Vedanta is the school of thought associated most closely with **Śaṅkara** (8th century CE), who argued that there is only one reality — **Brahman** — and that the apparent multiplicity of the world is *Maya*, not illusion in the sense of non-existence, but illusion in the sense of being mistaken for ultimate reality.

## The core claim

The individual self (*Ātman*) and the universal ground (*Brahman*) are not two things. They are identical. The famous formula is:

> *Aham Brahmāsmi* — I am Brahman.

This is not a mystical aspiration but a metaphysical claim about what always already is the case.

## Where I am with this

I came to Advaita through [Jeyamohan's essays](/notes/jeyamohan), particularly his reading of the Mahabharata. What struck me first was how the non-dual framework dissolves the problem of theodicy — if there is only Brahman, the question of why God permits evil stops making sense as asked.

The parallel with [Ibn Arabi's Wahdatul Wujud](/philosophy/ibn-arabi) is striking — both assert a radical ontological monism while preserving the phenomenal world as real at its own level.
