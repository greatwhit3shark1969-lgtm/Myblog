A Brick's Dream

philosophy

I haven't seen the movie, nor do I know the context of this scene — but from the comments, the movie may be a bit sus. 👀😷

Now, I pick it up from there.

Even a brick dreams of becoming something greater than itself. It dreams of being part of something greater, to transcend its material reality, to achieve an eternal presence.

For a brick to realize such a feat, it strives to be part of an architectural excellence — maybe a Renaissance Gothic Cathedral, a Chola Temple, or an Ottoman Mosque. It's the will to be a word in the Great Narration, to leave a lasting relevance, that drives the brick's existence.

Now, in the case of most bricks that came into this existence, most of them never achieve their dream and deteriorate into scattered particles of sand.

Before its baking, a brick is nothing more than individual particles of sand. It is this cosmic, perpetual combination that puts together a single, unique brick.

While it is, in fact, a magnanimous presence of forces and matter coming together in a special combination — a billion sand particles in a particular proportion, under a certain energy state — to bake a perfect brick. As the Brick Carl Sagan would put it: "We the Bricks are nothing but Stardust."

Yet the brick thinks of itself as an individual entity, an isolated presence.

Nevertheless, the energy eventually does fall apart into bits and pieces — particles of sand. This whole universe is one brahmmanda dance of particles, crashing into each other to make sweet moments of contact, only to drift apart and crash another and another… to ♾️

Here, the brick doesn't get any say in choosing its destiny. It never by itself manages to lay itself among the finest of monuments. It's the act of a higher sentient that decides whether the brick gets to make it in Qutub Minar or be used as a footstep in a puddle.

The only thing a brick can control — to a certain extent — is to hold itself together in its cuboid perfection, or let itself crumble into near-infinite pieces by daily friction.

But no matter how many times a brick gets disintegrated into dust, all the dust eventually does end up resurrected as bricks.

Now, this raises the question:

What makes a brick, a brick?
